# Generate product proposal

A product proposal answers: should we build a new thing? Unlike a feature proposal it cannot borrow credibility from an existing product — it must argue that demand is real and timing is right. The risk of a product proposal is that both the problem and the solution are being assumed; the proposal must surface those assumptions explicitly.

## How to run it

1. Ask: who is the user, and what is the evidence that this problem is real for them? Anecdote is fine to start, but the proposal must be honest about what is validated vs. assumed.
2. Ask: why now? Product proposals live or die on timing — capability shifts, cost changes, competitive moves, regulatory openings. If the answer is "it's a good idea," that is not a timing argument.
3. Ask: what alternatives exist that a user could choose today, and why would they choose this instead? If no alternatives exist, that is either a greenfield opportunity or a signal that demand is weaker than assumed.
4. Ask: what does success look like in 12 months? Adoption, revenue, validated learning — be specific.
5. Draft the proposal. The "why now" and "alternatives considered" sections are the hardest to write well; spend time on them.

## Emphasis for this type

- **Why now** — the single most important differentiator from a feature proposal. A product needs a timing argument: what changed that makes this viable or urgent now?
- **Alternatives considered** — name the specific alternatives a user could choose today. If this is a new category, explain why the category did not exist before and what changed.
- **Problem / motivation** — frame around the user's world, not the builder's vision. State what evidence exists and what is being assumed.
- **Success criteria** — include adoption or demand signal, not only technical completion.

Keep brief:
- **Scope & non-goals** — useful but not the load-bearing section here; a v1 cut is enough.
