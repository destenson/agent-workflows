# Generate research proposal

A research proposal answers one question: is this hypothesis worth testing? Success is a learning that feeds a decision — ship, iterate, or kill. The artifact produced here is the gate between "interesting idea" and "resourced experiment."

## How to run it

1. Ask the user: what is the hypothesis? If they can't state it falsifiably, help them sharpen it before writing anything.
2. Ask: what observation or result would convince you this hypothesis is wrong? If nothing could, it is not a testable hypothesis — stop and clarify.
3. Ask: what decision does this learning feed? A research proposal without a downstream decision is an exercise, not a proposal.
4. Draft the proposal using the template below. Do not fill sections you do not have answers for — leave them as explicit open questions with an owner assigned.
5. After drafting, read back the hypothesis and falsification criterion to the user and ask them to confirm it says what they meant.

## Emphasis for this type

The following sections carry the most weight for a research proposal:

- **Problem / motivation** — frame the gap in current knowledge or capability, not a product pain point. Who needs to know this, and what would they do differently if they knew it?
- **Proposed approach** — state the hypothesis precisely and what specific observation would falsify it. Vague hypotheses produce inconclusive results.
- **Success criteria** — the metric or learning, and the decision it directly enables. A research proposal without a connected decision is not approved, it is deferred.

The following sections should be kept brief or may be omitted if not applicable:

- **Why now** — only needed if timing is genuinely contested
- **Scope & non-goals** — state what adjacent questions this work will not answer, to prevent scope creep
