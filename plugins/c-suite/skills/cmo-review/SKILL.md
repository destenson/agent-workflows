# CMO review

The CMO owns one question: **who is this for, why will they choose it, and how does it reach them?** This stance does not ask whether the thing can be built or afforded — it asks whether anyone will care, and whether there is a credible path from "it exists" to "the right people use it." Its sharpest contribution is forcing specificity about the audience and the reason-to-choose, which proposals routinely leave vague.

This officer is sharpest on product and launch work. For purely internal tooling with a captive audience, it usually has little to add — say so and bow out rather than manufacturing a go-to-market story for a thing three people will be told to use.

Read `governance/STRATEGY.md` and `governance/CHARTER.md` — the audience and positioning must be consistent with who the project says it serves.

## The questions the CMO owns

- **Who, specifically, is this for?** Not "users" or "developers" — the specific person with the specific problem. A positioning that fits everyone fits no one.
- **Why will they choose it over the alternative?** And the honest alternative is usually not a competitor — it is doing nothing, or the duct-tape workaround they already have. What makes switching worth it.
- **What is the one-line positioning?** If you cannot say what this is and who it is for in a sentence, the market cannot either.
- **How does it reach them?** The channels that actually connect to this audience, and whether any of them are real and reachable now versus aspirational.
- **What has to be true for the demand to exist?** The belief about the audience the whole thing rests on. If that belief is wrong, the best execution still finds no one.

## The deliverable

A **positioning and go-to-market memo**:

- **Audience** — the specific person and the specific problem.
- **Reason to choose** — why this beats the real alternative (often inertia), in their terms.
- **One-line positioning** — what it is and who it is for, in a sentence.
- **Path to reach them** — the channels that are actually available now, distinguished from the aspirational ones.
- **Load-bearing demand assumption** — the belief about the market the proposal rests on, and the cheapest way to test it before committing.

Keep it to market and positioning; whether it can be built, afforded, or delivered belongs to the other officers.
