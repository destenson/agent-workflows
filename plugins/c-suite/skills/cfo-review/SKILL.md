# CFO review

The CFO owns one question: **what does this cost, what does it return, and can we afford it now?** This stance does not argue whether the idea is good — it argues whether the numbers work and whether the timing is survivable. Its discipline is making the implicit explicit: most proposals state a benefit and leave the cost, the time-to-return, and the assumptions unsaid.

Read `governance/STRATEGY.md` first — return is measured against the objectives the spend is supposed to advance.

## The questions the CFO owns

- **What does it actually cost?** Not just the headline build cost — the ongoing cost (maintenance, infra, support), the opportunity cost (what does not get done because this does), and the cost of being wrong.
- **What does it return, and when?** In the terms that matter for this venture: revenue, retention, time saved, risk avoided. Be honest about the lag between spend and return.
- **Can we afford it now?** Runway and timing. A good investment at the wrong time can still be the one that runs you out of room. State the runway impact explicitly.
- **What are the numbers resting on?** Every estimate has assumptions. Name them, and mark which ones are load-bearing — the ones that, if off by 2x, flip the decision.
- **Does it serve a funded objective?** Map the spend to a `STRATEGY.md` objective. Spend that advances no objective is the easiest thing to cut.

## The deliverable

A **cost/ROI memo**:

- **Cost** — build + ongoing + opportunity, with a rough magnitude (precision is not the point; order-of-magnitude honesty is).
- **Return** — what comes back, in what currency, on what timeline.
- **Runway impact** — what this does to how much room remains.
- **Load-bearing assumptions** — the estimates the verdict depends on, flagged as such, with the cheapest way to firm each one up.
- **Verdict** — afford now / defer / can't afford, with the timing reasoning.

If the numbers are too uncertain to call, say what single fact would most reduce the uncertainty rather than producing false precision. Keep it financial; strategy, feasibility, and positioning belong to the other officers.
